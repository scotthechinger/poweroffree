About the Report

When calls are free, everything changes.

For decades, a $1.5 billion prison telecom industry has extracted wealth from families who can least afford it — charging exorbitant rates for a phone call and costing families nearly $4,200 a year in incarceration-related expenses. Black families have borne a disproportionate share, paying 2.5 times more than white families for the same connection.

A growing movement has changed that. This report represents the first systematic effort to document what happens when the cost barrier is removed: drawing on quantitative data and in-depth qualitative interviews across multiple jurisdictions, it presents direct evidence of the profound impact free communication has on incarcerated people, their families, and the facilities that hold them.

Key Findings

Eight areas of measurable impact

Drawing on structured data and in-depth interviews across multiple jurisdictions, the report identifies eight areas where free communication has produced documented, meaningful change — for incarcerated people, their families, and the systems that hold them.

When the cost barrier is removed, the most immediate change is simple: people talk more. In Connecticut, average phone usage surged from 18 to 43 minutes per person per day — a 142% increase. In California, usage tripled. On average, incarcerated people spoke 45 minutes per day after free calls, compared to 25 minutes before — a 79% increase.

330,000+
people now have access to free calls — enabling nearly 600 million additional calls and 6.4 billion additional minutes of connection to date

I'm not looking at a clock saying, how much did that cost them today.

— Mike, Currently incarcerated, New York

This isn't only a quantitative shift. More frequent contact means relationships can be maintained in real time — birthdays, school updates, crises, and everyday moments that sustain human bonds across prison walls.

What had once been rationed and rushed became something else: ordinary, ongoing presence. In jails, average daily minutes per person more than doubled, from 27 to 57 — an even steeper jump, reflecting the urgent needs of people recently incarcerated and those awaiting trial.

1:12

6.4 billion minutes restored — and counting

Families with incarcerated loved ones — disproportionately low-income, disproportionately Black and brown — have shouldered the cost of a $1.5 billion prison telecom industry for decades. One in three goes into debt to stay connected, and women carry 87% of that burden.

$622.5M
shifted back to families to date — with 73% flowing to non-white families, who have borne this cost most heavily

Then it clicked, that I don't have to put money on the phone anymore. I started crying because it was just such a relief. That's not something I ever have to worry about again.

— Nia, Wife of an incarcerated man, Massachusetts

Savings per family ranged from $172 per year in California to $1,801 in Connecticut. Families used those dollars to cover housing, reduce debt, and plan for the future. For some, it meant the first real financial breathing room since their loved one was incarcerated.

The financial shift had emotional dimensions too. Incarcerated people described the shame of asking family to fund their calls — a shame that evaporated overnight. When the cost disappeared, so did a persistent source of guilt on both sides of the line.

1:08

$622.5 million back in families' hands

Free communication creates a fundamental shift from transactional to relational. When the constant calculation — whether connection is worth the cost — disappears, conversations become something else entirely. People sit on the phone while a child does homework, share prayer, watch movies together, listen to each other do the dishes.

93%
of incarcerated people and 82% of family members said free calls helped build or repair relationships — the most common theme across the entire interview dataset

Any loved one or family member is doing the sentence together. I need to feel [my husband] in my life, so that there is hope.

— Brashari, Wife of an incarcerated man, Massachusetts

Participants described rekindling relationships that had broken — sometimes after years of silence. Hearing a daughter's voice for the first time in a decade. Meeting grandchildren. Being present for a birthday surprise over the phone.

These connections don't just sustain people through incarceration. They are the foundation on which successful return home is built — the relationships that incarcerated people will rely on after release to navigate housing, employment, and belonging.

1:15

When the clock stops, real conversation begins

For children with incarcerated parents, phone calls are not a luxury — they are a necessity for healthy development and emotional security. When calls were costly, parents missed the small, consistent moments that build trust: waking children up, checking in before school, helping with homework, saying goodnight.

Nearly half
of people in state prisons are parents to minor children — 19% with children four years old or younger

You can't force a child to say what's going on in 30 minutes. But you can hear if your child is going through something by the tone of their voice. With free phone calls, if she has something going on, she can talk to me.

— Natavia, Currently incarcerated, New York

Free communication restored those moments. Parents describe children running to the phone, eagerly anticipating calls, opening up in ways that shorter, rationed conversations never allowed. For children with disabilities, the impact was especially significant — consistent daily contact produced measurable developmental gains.

These effects help mitigate the intergenerational harm of parental incarceration in concrete, documented ways. Research shows that stronger parent-child relationships during incarceration improve outcomes for impacted children — and the generations that come after.

1:04

What it means to raise a child over the phone

Prisons and jails are isolating by design. Incarcerated people cut off from their families experience higher rates of depression, anxiety, and deteriorating physical health. Free communication functions as a lifeline — providing the human connection that is protective against the psychological toll of incarceration.

76%
of incarcerated people, 77% of family members, and 100% of correctional staff described improvements in stress, emotional stability, or overall wellbeing once calls became free

Without the free phone calls, all your connections are cut off. You are literally isolated from everything. You get a sense of hope when you can still interact with people on the outside and see there's people out there that care about you.

— Robert, Formerly incarcerated, California

Participants described being able to grieve alongside loved ones, process difficult news in real time, and regulate emotion simply by having someone to call. The benefits extend to families on the outside, who experience relief from both the financial and emotional strain of rationed contact.

Inside facilities, individual improvements in mental health ripple outward — a calmer person is a safer person. Free communication also opened an unexpected medical channel: when incarcerated people couldn't get adequate attention from correctional staff, loved ones could call the facility and escalate concerns on their behalf.

1:09

Connection as medicine

Phones have long been the most volatile pressure point inside correctional facilities — the place where people receive the best and worst news, where financial scarcity and emotional charge collide. Before free calling, participants described long lines, bullying over phone access, and conversations cut short by cost, sending people back into shared living spaces still carrying unresolved stress.

100%
of corrections staff interviewed described free calls as a security tool that reduced tensions and made their jobs easier

When they're having a good day, we're having a good day.

— Officer Walls, Corrections Officer, New York

Free communication and personal tablets removed the scarcity that drove that conflict. Seventy-nine percent of incarcerated people described positive changes in the prison environment. Research supports this: expanded communication is associated with a 27% reduction in disciplinary infractions and roughly a 20% reduction in violent incidents.

Correctional staff, incarcerated people, and family members alike used the same word: calmer. With fewer conflicts and increased support from loved ones in navigating emotionally charged situations, disciplinary infractions dropped — and with them, barriers to early release.

1:06

A phone call as a security tool

Free communication doesn't replace rehabilitation programs — it activates them. When incarcerated people maintain consistent contact with loved ones, they regain a sense of identity and responsibility that motivates genuine engagement in their own growth. Hope is not abstract. It comes from specific people who are waiting.

Connection
as the root of rehabilitation — maintained family ties correlate with reduced disciplinary infractions, increased program engagement, and earlier release

Conversations create a pathway for empathy and healing — and actually, just hope for a future when I get out. Knowing that I have people that I can go to. Without these phone calls, that would not have been possible.

— Jasmeil, Currently incarcerated, California

Participants describe a measurable internal shift: a reason to exercise, to enroll in programs, to stay out of trouble. Fewer disciplinary infractions often directly affect parole eligibility. The civic ties sustained through free calls — to faith communities, advocacy networks, and mutual aid — lay groundwork for reintegration before release ever happens.

As the report puts it: these connections are not separate from rehabilitation. They may be the root of it. Maintaining community ties, contributing to shared efforts, and staying engaged in collective life reinforce the habits, relationships, and sense of purpose that support long-term change.

1:10

Hope has a phone number

Reentry is daunting. Formerly incarcerated people face homelessness at nearly 10 times the general population rate and unemployment above 27%. Overburdened case managers cannot close that gap alone. What research consistently shows is that strong, sustained community ties are among the most powerful predictors of successful return.

42%
of incarcerated people spend their first free night at the home of a family member or friend — and 54% of those employed after release used personal connections to find that job

I was able to call social services, call transitional housing, call the school. I got out in December, and in January, I was in school. All of that was possible because we didn't have to pay for the phone.

— Donald Diggs, Formerly incarcerated, California

Free communication lets reentry planning begin before the prison door opens. Participants describe using calls to secure housing, arrange employment, enroll in education, coordinate treatment — all before release. The bonds built through consistent connection during incarceration become the foundation for reintegration.

Successful reentry is not just a personal outcome — it is a public one. When people return home with stable housing, employment, and strong support networks, communities are safer and public systems are less burdened. Free communication is not simply a benefit to incarcerated people and their families; it is an investment in the communities they return to.

About This Project

Critical Connections

The Power of Free is a project of Worth Rises, a non-profit organization dedicated to dismantling the prison industry and ending the exploitation of those it touches.

This report and the accompanying films were produced in partnership with directly impacted communities — the people whose lives are shaped by these policies and whose voices are central to this work.

The research, interviews, and data in this project reflect years of advocacy, scholarship, and on-the-ground organizing by families, advocates, and researchers committed to a more just system.

About the Report

When calls are free, everything changes.

For decades, a $1.5 billion prison telecom industry has extracted wealth from families who can least afford it — charging exorbitant rates for a phone call and costing families nearly $4,200 a year in incarceration-related expenses. Black families have borne a disproportionate share, paying 2.5 times more than white families for the same connection.

A growing movement has changed that. This report represents the first systematic effort to document what happens when the cost barrier is removed: drawing on quantitative data and in-depth qualitative interviews across multiple jurisdictions, it presents direct evidence of the profound impact free communication has on incarcerated people, their families, and the facilities that hold them.

Key Findings

Eight areas of measurable impact

Drawing on structured data and in-depth interviews across multiple jurisdictions, the report identifies eight areas where free communication has produced documented, meaningful change — for incarcerated people, their families, and the systems that hold them.

When the cost barrier is removed, the most immediate change is simple: people talk more. In Connecticut, average phone usage surged from 18 to 43 minutes per person per day — a 142% increase. In California, usage tripled, from 14 to 44 minutes per person per day. On average, incarcerated people spoke 51 minutes per day after free calls, compared to 28 minutes before.

104% increase in average monthly call minutes across five state prison systems (118.5M to 242.2M)

This isn't just a quantitative shift. More frequent contact means relationships can be maintained in real time — birthdays, school updates, crises, and everyday moments that sustain human bonds across prison walls.

"I can call you every single day if I want to now. Previously it may have been once a week or every few weeks. So I feel a part of the everyday life of the people that I love and the people that love me."

— Pamela Smart, Currently incarcerated, New York

Increased Connection — Short documentary

Families with incarcerated loved ones — disproportionately low-income communities and communities of color — have shouldered billions in communication costs. The prison telecom industry, a $1.5 billion sector controlled largely by three companies, has extracted wealth from families who can least afford it. One in three families goes into debt to stay connected, and women carry 87% of that burden.

$105.3M saved annually Annual savings across all prisons and jails studied from free communications

In California, free calls save families $16.1 million per year in prisons alone. Ninety percent of interview respondents described the financial impact of free calls as transformative — dollars that once went to telecom companies now go toward housing, food, and rebuilding lives.

"It's already bad enough that he's there. So to have them taking money out of your pocket — just so that I can speak to my son — only worsens the whole situation."

— Shelene, Mother of two incarcerated sons, Minnesota

Financial Relief — Short documentary

Free communication creates a fundamental shift from transactional to relational. When the constant calculation — whether connection is worth the cost — disappears, conversations become truly meaningful rather than rushed and rationed. People are able to participate in family celebrations, offer guidance through difficult moments, and maintain the emotional fabric that incarceration strains.

93% of incarcerated people and 82% of family members identified building and repairing relationships as a major impact of free calls

These slower, more conversational talks serve as a source of healing and hope — providing the kind of presence that sustains people through the hardships of separation.

"Those free phone calls keep me in constant contact with her. I would call her like every six months before that because our call was 20-something dollars. She always looked forward to hearing my voice."

— Gregory Pierre, Currently incarcerated, Connecticut

Family & Community — Short documentary

For children with incarcerated parents, phone calls are not a luxury — they are a necessity for healthy development and emotional security. When calls were costly, children experienced forced disconnection at critical moments in their lives. Free communications allow incarcerated parents to participate in homework help, school events, and daily check-ins that sustain the parental bond.

Daily contact restored Incarcerated parents can now speak with their children every day, not just when finances allow

The ability to maintain consistent parental presence — even across prison walls — has measurable impacts on child development, school performance, and the child's sense of security and belonging.

"I remember calling my daughter so early in the morning that she wouldn't talk. She would wake up, pick up the phone and say, mm-hmm. What the phone is — it's me there. It's my presence. It's her having her father."

— Michael Tineo, Currently incarcerated, New York

Parenting & Child Development — Short documentary

Free communication serves as a powerful rehabilitation tool. When incarcerated people maintain consistent contact with loved ones, they experience greater motivation to participate in educational and self-improvement programs. Hope itself becomes a catalyst for change — knowing there is a support network waiting creates tangible accountability.

Higher program engagement Maintained family connections correlate with increased participation in education and vocational programs

The ability to share achievements and personal growth with loved ones in real time reinforces positive changes and creates the kind of motivation that sustains transformation over years of incarceration.

"Conversations create a pathway for empathy and healing and just a healthy relationship with my family — and actually, just hope for a future when I get out. Knowing that I have people that I can go to. Without these phone calls, that would not have been possible."

— Jasmeil, Currently incarcerated, California

Rehabilitation — Short documentary

Facilities that have implemented free calling consistently report reductions in violent incidents and disciplinary infractions. Both staff and incarcerated people describe the change as immediate and palpable. When people have something to protect — a call with their child, a conversation with a partner — behavior changes.

100% of corrections staff interviewed described free calls as reducing tension and making their jobs easier

The ability to talk to families reduces the stress and isolation that fuels violence. When people are no longer desperate about maintaining costly connections, they are less likely to engage in the behaviors that lead to disciplinary infractions — creating safer environments for everyone, including correctional officers.

"It's helping to keep them calm and keeping them level-headed, a little grounded. Even if it's not always good news, at least they know almost immediately what's going on with their families."

— Officer Walls, Corrections Officer, Connecticut

Reduced Violence — Short documentary

Isolation is a health crisis. Incarcerated people cut off from their families experience higher rates of depression, anxiety, and physical health deterioration. Free communication functions as a lifeline — providing the kind of human connection that is protective against the psychological toll of incarceration.

76% of incarcerated people 77% of family members, and 100% of staff described emotional and mental health improvements from free calls

The mental health benefits extend to families as well, who experience relief from the constant financial and emotional strain of maintaining connections. Physical health also improves through reduced violence exposure and lower stress-related illness.

"And sometimes I just needed to cry. I needed somebody to fill me up. I needed somebody to give me inspiration and guide me."

— Yannick Ortega, Formerly incarcerated, California
1:09

Mental & Physical Health — Short documentary

People who maintain strong family connections during incarceration are far more likely to have stable housing, employment, and support upon release. Free communication allows reentry planning to begin before the prison door opens — securing housing, arranging employment, and maintaining relationships with mentors and family members who will support the transition home.

Stronger networks Maintained connections are the leading predictor of successful reentry and reduced recidivism

The bonds built through consistent, free communication during incarceration become the foundation for successful community reintegration — turning the moment of release from a crisis into a homecoming.

"I was able to call social services, call the program to help me get right into school. I got out in December — in January I was in school. All of that was possible because we didn't have to pay for the phone."

— Donald Diggs, Formerly incarcerated, Connecticut

Connecticut

The state that started it all

91%
reduction in cost per minute

Connecticut became the first state in the country to make all prison and jail communication free — including phone calls, video calls, and electronic messaging — when Senate Bill 972 took effect in July 2022. The policy affects more than 10,000 incarcerated people and guarantees a minimum of 90 minutes of call time per day.

The state renegotiated its contract with Securus, moving to a per-person pricing model that dropped the effective rate from 25.8 cents to 2.3 cents per minute — the largest cost reduction of any state studied.

The victory followed years of organizing by Worth Rises and the Connecting Families Connecticut coalition, championed in the legislature by State Representative Josh Elliott and powered by directly impacted families like Diane Lewis, who became one of the campaign's most powerful advocates.

California's Keep Families Connected Act made phone calls free across all state prisons and youth detention facilities starting January 1, 2023. The policy built on earlier victories in San Francisco, San Diego, and Los Angeles, where local advocates had already eliminated jail call costs.

The state renegotiated its contract with ViaPath and introduced calling-enabled tablets system-wide, securing a volume discount that brought the per-minute cost down to as low as 1.9 cents. Average monthly call minutes tripled — from 41.6 million to 125.0 million — and average daily usage per person rose from 14 to 44 minutes.

Worth Rises and the Keep Families Connected coalition, including the San Francisco Financial Justice Project, Essie Justice Group, and Young Women's Freedom Center, built steady pressure that brought the bill across the finish line with legislative champion State Senator Josh Becker.

New York took a different path. Rather than waiting for legislation, the Department of Corrections and Community Supervision made all state prison calls free administratively — incorporating communication costs directly into the corrections budget. The policy took effect August 1, 2025, going from decision to implementation in days rather than months.

The state negotiated the lowest per-minute rate in the country at just 1.5 cents — well below federal rate caps and comparable policies in other states. Every person in New York state prisons now has their own tablet for making calls, with no limits on usage. Average daily call minutes per person rose from 64 to 88, the highest sustained level observed in any system studied.

The administrative approach offers a model for states where the legislative process has proven slow or politically difficult. Worth Rises spearheaded the campaign with leaders including Assembly Speaker Carl Heastie, Assembly Member Harvey Epstein, and State Senator Jamaal T. Bailey.

About This Project

Critical Connections

The Power of Free is a project of Worth Rises, a non-profit organization dedicated to dismantling the prison industry and ending the exploitation of those it touches.

This report and the accompanying films were produced in partnership with directly impacted communities — the people whose lives are shaped by these policies and whose voices are central to this work.

The research, interviews, and data in this project reflect years of advocacy, scholarship, and on-the-ground organizing by families, advocates, and researchers committed to a more just system.
